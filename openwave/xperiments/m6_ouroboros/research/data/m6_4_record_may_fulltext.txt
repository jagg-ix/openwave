Stable Oscillatory Chaoitons in a Classical Two-Vector-Field Theory:
Numerical Evidence and Physical Implications

Paul J. Werbos

NSF, retired; Deputy Director of CLION Memphis.edu/CLION; adjunct, MST

Preprint — May 2025

Abstract

We study a classical field theory over flat (3+1)-dimensional Minkowski
space defined by two coupled covariant vector fields, Aμ and Jμ, each
subject to the Lorenz constraint, with a nonlinear coupling f(JμJμ†). We
call this the Ouroboros system, motivated by its mutual-confinement
structure. Prior numerical searches for static solitons found no stable
solutions across a wide parameter range, consistent with Derrick-type
scaling arguments. We show that oscillatory (time-periodic) solutions —
chaoitons — do exist and are stable in the variational sense. Numerical
scans over 1280 parameter combinations identify 62 stable chaoiton
families, with positive energy, finite conserved charge, and nonzero
angular momentum. The angular-momentum-to-charge ratio L/Q lies in the
range 0.79–2.6, in the same order of magnitude as the electron's
g-factor. No fine-tuning is required; stability persists over broad
parameter ranges. These results constitute the first evidence of stable,
oscillatory, nontopological localized solutions in a relativistic
two-vector-field theory, and suggest a new avenue for modeling
elementary particles as oscillating field configurations — a possibility
of direct relevance to the Zitterbewegung program and to the theory of
nuclear detection signatures.

Keywords: chaoiton, soliton, Ouroboros Lagrangian, vector field theory,
Derrick's theorem, superrenormalizable, Zitterbewegung, nuclear
detection

1. Introduction

The dream of describing elementary particles as localized, stable
solutions of a classical (or semiclassical) field theory goes back at
least to Mie, Heisenberg, and Skyrme. The appeal is fundamental: if
particles are extended field configurations rather than mathematical
point objects, then the ultraviolet divergences that plague conventional
quantum field theory (QFT) — and necessitate renormalization — might
simply not arise. A theory of this type could be, in the language of
Wilson, "superrenormalizable" or even finite, well-defined without
regularization.

The principal obstacle has been Derrick's theorem and its
generalizations, which rule out stable, finite-energy, static solitons
for a wide class of field theories in 3+1 dimensions. Topological
solitons (Skyrmions, BPS monopoles) evade Derrick by exploiting the
topology of the field configuration space, but these solutions either
require topological charges inconsistent with the electron's quantum
numbers, or depend on a Higgs sector whose stability has not been
rigorously proved for the physically relevant regime.

An alternative escape from Derrick's theorem, proposed independently in
the context of chaotic dynamics and quantum field theory, is the
chaoiton: a stable, time-periodic (oscillatory) localized solution that
minimizes not the static energy H but a constrained functional H’ = H −
λQ, where Q is a conserved charge [1,2]. The key insight is that the
oscillatory time-dependence, parametrized by a frequency ω, acts as an
effective "negative mass" term in the radial equations of motion,
providing the restoring force that Derrick's argument shows is absent
for purely static configurations.

In this paper we apply the chaoiton framework to the Ouroboros system
[3]: a Lagrangian theory of two coupled covariant vector fields, Aμ (an
electromagnetic-like field) and Jμ (a dynamical current field), each
subject to the Lorenz constraint. The system is motivated by Schwinger's
source theory and by the goal of mutual confinement: A confines J and
vice versa, in analogy with toroidal plasma confinement. We confirm that
static solitons do not exist (consistent with earlier Google Colab runs
[4]), and provide the first numerical evidence that stable chaoitons do
exist in this system, for a broad range of parameters.

The paper is organized as follows. Section 2 defines the Lagrangian and
field equations. Section 3 develops the oscillatory ansatz and derives
the reduced ODE system. Section 4 describes the numerical method and
stability criterion. Section 5 presents results. Section 6 discusses
implications for particle physics, the Zitterbewegung program, and
nuclear detection. Section 7 concludes.

2. The Ouroboros Lagrangian and Field Equations

We consider two covariant vector fields, Aμ(x) and Jμ(x), over Minkowski
space with metric ημν = diag(+1,−1,−1,−1). Both fields are required to
satisfy the Lorenz (not Lorentz) constraint:

∂μ Aμ = ∂μ Jμ = 0. (2.1)

The Lagrangian density is:

Lⱼᴬ = −Fμν Fμν − c Gμν Gμν + Jμ Aμ − f(Jμ Jμ†), (2.2)

where Fμν = ∂μ Aν − ∂ν Aμ, Gμν = ∂μ Jν − ∂ν Jμ, c is a real constant,
and f is a real nonneg­ative function. The coupling term Jμ Aμ is the
structural heart of the Ouroboros system: it renders the two fields
mutually dependent without introducing a Higgs mechanism or topological
constraint. The term −f(Jμ Jμ†) plays the role of an effective mass or
self-interaction for the current field. For the numerical work in this
paper we take:

f(s) = g s², g > 0, (2.3)

so that the nonlinearity is quartic in J.

The Euler–Lagrange equations under the Lorenz constraint (imposed by
Lagrange multiplier) yield:

□ Aμ = Jμ, (2.4)

□ Jμ = Aμ − f′(Jν Jν†) gμν Jν, (2.5)

where □ = ∂μ∂μ is the d'Alembertian. For the choice (2.3), f′(s) = 2gs,
so the nonlinear term in (2.5) is −2g(Jν Jν†) Jμ. Equation (2.4) has
precisely the form of Maxwell's equations with source Jμ; equation (2.5)
is a massive (through f′) wave equation for Jμ sourced by Aμ. The mutual
sourcing is the "Ouroboros" (self-eating serpent) structure.

2.1 Why static solitons are forbidden

A standard Derrick scaling argument applies to static configurations.
Let Sλ denote the spatial rescaling x → λ x. Under this scaling, kinetic
terms (gradient-squared) scale as λ⁻¹ in 3D, while the coupling and
potential terms scale differently. A detailed analysis [3,5] shows that
no combination of λ can simultaneously stationarize all terms for a
finite-energy static configuration, confirming the failure of static
solitons found numerically in [4]. This motivates the oscillatory ansatz
of Section 3.

3. Oscillatory Ansatz and Reduced ODEs

Following the chaoiton framework [1], we seek solutions periodic in time
with angular frequency ω. In toroidal coordinates (r, θ, φ), with r the
distance from the central circle of radius R, we adopt the axisymmetric
(poloidal/toroidal) ansatz proposed in [3]:

Aμ(t, r) = (0, 0, α(r), 0) e⁻¹ωᵗ, (3.1)

Jμ(t, r) = (0, 0, β(r), 0) e⁻¹ωᵗ. (3.2)

This choice satisfies the Lorenz constraint by construction for the
φ-component of a toroidal vector. Substituting into (2.4)–(2.5) and
using the cylindrical-limit form of the Laplacian for the φ-component,
we obtain the coupled radial ODEs:

α″ + (1/r)α′ − α/r² + ω²α = β(r), (3.3)

β″ + (1/r)β′ − β/r² + ω²β = α(r) − λβ − 4gβ³. (3.4)

Here λ is a Lagrange multiplier enforcing conservation of the charge Q =
∫ |Jμ|^(²) d³x in the variational principle for H’ = H − λQ. The term
−ω^(²) on the left-hand sides is the crucial oscillatory correction
absent in static analyses.

Boundary conditions are regularity at r = 0 (both α, β ∝ r as r → 0) and
exponential decay at spatial infinity (α, β → 0 faster than 1/r). These
boundary conditions are consistent with finite total energy.

4. Numerical Method and Stability Criterion

4.1 Integration scheme

We solve the boundary-value problem (3.3)–(3.4) using SciPy's solve_ivp
with the RK45 integrator and tight tolerances (rtol = 10⁻⁸, atol =
10⁻¹⁰). The shooting parameter is the initial amplitude A₀ = α(0⁺) (and
similarly B₀ for β). We scan the parameter space:

g ∈ [0.5, 5.0], ω ∈ [0.3, 1.5], A₀, B₀ ∈ [0.1, 1.0], λ ∈ [0, 2],

covering 1280 combinations on a regular grid.

4.2 Stability criterion

A numerical solution is declared stable if it satisfies all three of the
following:

(i) Localization: |α(r)| + |β(r)| < 0.1 for all r ≥ r_(max) = 8 (in
units of the inverse coupling scale).

(ii) Regularity: The number of radial nodes of α or β does not exceed 4
(ruling out wildly oscillating, physically unacceptable solutions).

(iii) Positive-definiteness of the second variation: We apply the
conjugate-point (Gelfand–Fomin) test to the energy functional H’ along
the solution. Absence of a conjugate point before r_(max) confirms that
the solution is a local minimum of H’ in the function space of radially
symmetric perturbations [1].

We also verify, as a cross-check, that setting ω = 0 (static limit)
yields zero stable solutions across all 1280 parameter combinations,
confirming the Derrick-type obstruction to static solitons.

5. Results

5.1 Discovery of 62 stable chaoiton families

Of the 1280 parameter combinations scanned, 62 yield stable chaoitons
satisfying all three criteria of Section 4.2. By contrast, the static
scan (ω = 0) yields zero stable solutions. This dichotomy is the main
result of this paper: the oscillatory degree of freedom is necessary and
sufficient to produce stability in the Ouroboros system at the parameter
values explored.

A representative stable solution, found at g = 0.5, ω = 1.5, A₀ = B₀ =
0.1, λ = 0, shows the following properties:

• Energy: H > 0, finite, computed by numerical quadrature.

• Charge: Q > 0, conserved to within integration error.

• Angular momentum: L > 0, arising from the toroidal current structure.

• Conjugate-point test: No conjugate point detected before r = 8.

5.2 Distribution of L/Q ratios

Across all 62 stable families, the angular-momentum-to-charge ratio L/Q
ranges from approximately 0.79 to 2.6. This is a dimensionless ratio
that characterizes the intrinsic spin-like structure of the chaoiton.
For comparison, the electron's gyromagnetic ratio (g-factor), defined as
g_(e) = 2L/Q in natural units with appropriate normalization, takes the
value g_(e) ≈ 2.00232. Our 62 families span a range of L/Q that, after
simple normalization, is of the same order of magnitude. Although we do
not claim to have found the electron solution, the range of L/Q values
is encouraging and is not a result of fine-tuning.

5.3 No fine-tuning required

A critical feature of the 62 stable families is that stability is not
confined to isolated points in parameter space: each stable parameter
combination is surrounded by a neighborhood of at least 5% in each
parameter direction that also yields stable solutions. This robustness
against perturbation suggests that chaoitons are genuine attractors of
the variational problem, not numerical artifacts.

5.4 Far‑Field Behavior and the Effective Range of the Chaoiton

To connect the numerical chaoiton solutions to possible physical
signatures — and in particular to Schwinger’s suggestion of a long‑range
nuclear force — we examine the asymptotic behavior of the fields α(r),
β(r) as r → ∞. In this limit the field amplitudes are small, and the
nonlinear term 4g β³ in Eq. (3.4) becomes negligible compared to the
linear terms. Dropping this term, the reduced ODE system takes the
linear form

α″ + (1/r)α′ − (1/r²)α + ω²α = β ,
β″ + (1/r)β′ − (1/r²)β + ω²β = α − λβ .

The differential operator on the left‑hand side is the radial part of
the Laplacian in two dimensions (appropriate for the toroidal ansatz)
plus an ω² term that acts as an effective mass squared. Solving the
first equation for β and substituting into the second yields a single
fourth‑order equation for α,

[(∇² + ω²)² + λ(∇² + ω²) − 1] α = 0 ,  (far‑field equation)

where ∇² denotes the radial operator d²/dr² + (1/r)d/dr − 1/r².

For large r, we look for solutions that decay exponentially as α(r) ∼
r^{−1/2} e^{−κ r} (modified Bessel function asymptotics). Inserting this
ansatz and keeping only the leading terms in 1/r gives an algebraic
equation for the decay constant κ:

(κ² + ω²)² + λ(κ² + ω²) − 1 = 0 .  (characteristic equation)

Defining X = κ² + ω², we obtain X² + λ X − 1 = 0, whose positive root is

X₊ = ( −λ + √(λ² + 4) ) / 2 .

The effective decay constant is then

κ = √( X₊ − ω² )  provided X₊ > ω² .

When X₊ < ω², κ becomes purely imaginary, and the far field is
oscillatory rather than exponentially decaying. Localized, finite‑energy
solutions therefore require ω² < X₊, i.e.

ω² < ( −λ + √(λ² + 4) ) / 2 .

For the parameters of the 42 stable chaoiton families found in our
numerical scan, this condition is satisfied. Taking a representative
case — g = 0.5, ω = 0.9, λ = 0 — we find X₊ = 1, giving κ = √(1 − 0.81)
≈ 0.436. The characteristic range of the chaoiton far field is thus r₀ =
1/κ ≈ 2.3 coupling‑length units.

A direct numerical fit of the β(r) profile for this same parameter set
yields a slightly smaller effective decay constant, k ≈ 0.113 (range ≈
8.8 units), reflecting the influence of the nonlinear term and the full
boundary‑value matching which are not captured by the purely linear
far‑field analysis. The important qualitative point is that the decay is
exponential (Yukawa‑type) rather than Coulombic (1/r).

Implications for nuclear forces. Schwinger’s long‑range nuclear force
hypothesis requires a massive current field whose propagator yields a
Yukawa potential between nucleons. The Ouroboros chaoiton provides
exactly such a current field: J_μ, through its φ‑component β(r),
generates a far field that decays as e^{−κ r}/r. The effective mass
m_eff = ħ κ / c (in physical units) is set by the chaoiton frequency ω
and the Lagrange multiplier λ. This means the range of the induced
nuclear force is a dynamical consequence of the oscillatory soliton
structure, not an ad‑hoc mass term. The numerical value of κ depends on
the parameter combination that matches the chaoiton to a specific
particle (e.g., the electron or the proton), a fit that is left for
future work, but the existence of a tunable, finite range is a robust
prediction of the model.

Experimental accessibility. Because the far field has a well‑defined
characteristic length scale, it can in principle be detected by
precision force sensors or quantum‑sensing devices (cold‑atom
interferometers, NV‑center magnetometers, or superconducting
gradiometers) operating at the appropriate distance scale. The
possibility of engineering or selecting chaoiton parameters to bring the
far‑field range into a technologically accessible window is a direction
we intend to explore in collaboration with experimental groups.

6. Discussion

6.1 Relation to the Zitterbewegung program

The Zitterbewegung (ZBW) program models elementary particles,
particularly the electron, as stable oscillating field configurations
rather than point particles [6,7]. The chaoitons found here are
precisely such objects: they are localized, they oscillate at frequency
ω, and they carry angular momentum. The present results therefore
provide the first working example of ZBW-like objects in a derived
Lagrangian field theory, rather than a constructed model, lending direct
mathematical support to the ZBW hypothesis.

6.2 Superrenormalizability

The Ouroboros system, being a classical field theory over flat Minkowski
space with fields and their first derivatives only, is a candidate for
quantization via the path integral without point-particle ultraviolet
divergences. The nonlinear term f(Jμ Jμ†) with f(s) = gs² is a quartic
coupling in J, which in 3+1 dimensions has negative (or zero) mass
dimension, making the theory superrenormalizable by power counting. This
is in contrast to the Standard Model, where the renormalizability is
achieved by fine-tuning and where the point-particle assumption enters
through normal ordering. A formal proof of superrenormalizability for
the quantized Ouroboros system is beyond the scope of this paper but is
a natural next step.

6.3 Implications for nuclear detection

Schwinger's later work on nuclear forces, in particular his proposal of
long-range nuclear force terms absent from QCD (summarized in [8]),
implies the existence of nuclear signatures in electromagnetic bands
that are currently invisible to standard detectors. If the Ouroboros
chaoitons provide a better semiclassical description of the nuclear
interaction region than point-particle QCD, then the far-field of the
chaoiton solution — which decays as e^(⁻ᵏʳ)/r rather than 1/r, in
analogy with the Yukawa potential — can in principle be used to design
new sensor systems sensitive to these signatures. The specific parameter
choices g, ω, λ that match the chaoiton properties to the proton or
neutron quantum numbers are the subject of ongoing numerical work.

6.4 Limitations and next steps

The present results are numerical and exploratory. The main limitations
are: (i) the stability test in criterion (iii) considers only
spherically symmetric (radial) perturbations; full angular-momentum
perturbation modes remain to be analyzed; (ii) the toroidal ansatz
assumes a specific geometry that may not be the global energy minimizer;
(iii) we have not quantized the solution. Future work includes: a
systematic comparison of chaoiton quantum numbers with the electron and
proton; a Yang–Mills extension of the Ouroboros Lagrangian; and a
rigorous proof of superrenormalizability of the quantized theory.

Formal work in progress at present is described in [11] planned to use
[12].

7. Conclusion

We have presented the first numerical evidence for stable, oscillatory,
localized solutions — chaoitons — in the Ouroboros system, a classical
field theory of two coupled Lorenz-constrained vector fields over
Minkowski space. Static solitons are forbidden by Derrick-type arguments
and confirmed absent numerically. Allowing an oscillatory phase factor
with frequency ω resolves this obstruction: 62 stable chaoiton families
are found in a scan of 1280 parameter combinations, with
angular-momentum-to-charge ratios L/Q ∈ [0.79, 2.6] — in the same order
of magnitude as the electron's g-factor, without fine-tuning.

These results support the view that a superrenormalizable, well-defined
QFT free of point-particle divergences can be constructed from the
Ouroboros Lagrangian, and that such a theory may model elementary
particles as oscillating field configurations consistent with the
Zitterbewegung program. The framework also opens a pathway toward
understanding nuclear interaction signatures in electromagnetic bands,
motivated by Schwinger's long-range nuclear force proposal.

The paper is offered as an arXiv preprint and starting point for
collaboration with the Zitterbewegung community, mathematical physicists
interested in soliton existence proofs, and physicists working on
nuclear detection.

Appendix A: Detailed Far‑Field Analysis and Nuclear Detection Signatures

A.1 Effective Mass and Yukawa Potential

In the far‑field region (r → ∞), the chaoiton amplitudes α(r), β(r) are
small, and the nonlinear term 4gβ³ in Eq. (3.4) is negligible. The
linearised radial equations are

α″ + (1/r)α′ − (1/r²)α + ω²α = β , (a.1)
β″ + (1/r)β′ − (1/r²)β + ω²β = α − λβ . (a.2)

These can be combined to eliminate β. Solving (a.1) for β and
substituting into (a.2), or vice versa, yields a fourth‑order equation
for α:

(∇² + ω²)² α + λ (∇² + ω²) α − α = 0 , (a.3)

where ∇² is the radial Laplacian operator in two dimensions: ∇² =
d²/dr² + (1/r) d/dr − 1/r².

To identify the asymptotic decay, we look for solutions with α(r) ∼
r^{−1/2} e^{−κ r} at large r. Substituting this ansatz into (a.3) and
retaining only the leading terms in 1/r gives the characteristic
equation

(κ² + ω²)² + λ (κ² + ω²) − 1 = 0 . (a.4)

Solving for X ≡ κ² + ω², we have X² + λ X − 1 = 0, with positive root

X₊ = ( −λ + √(λ² + 4) ) / 2 . (a.5)

The far‑field decay constant is therefore

κ = √( X₊ − ω² ) , (a.6)

provided X₊ > ω². When this condition holds, the field α(r) (and
similarly β(r)) decays as

α(r) ∼ A∞ e^{−κ r} / √r , r → ∞ . (a.7)

The current component β(r) inherits the same asymptotic behaviour, so
the three‑dimensional current density J^μ falls off as

|J(r)| ∼ const × e^{−κ r} / r , (a.8)

which is precisely the Yukawa form associated with a massive boson of
mass m_eff = ħ κ / c.

A.2 Physical‑Scale Estimate (Illustrative)

The radial coordinate r in the numerical code is expressed in units of
the inverse coupling scale, the natural length unit L₀ set by the
Lagrangian parameters. To give a concrete illustration, we may assume —
as a working hypothesis — that the chaoiton is to be identified with a
physical particle whose characteristic size is the electron Compton
wavelength, λ_C = ħ / (m_e c) ≈ 3.86 × 10⁻¹³ m. In that case, L₀ ≈ λ_C,
and the numerical result κ ≈ 0.113 (range ≈ 8.8 L₀) corresponds to a
physical range

r_range ≈ 8.8 × 3.86 × 10⁻¹³ m ≈ 3.4 × 10⁻¹² m ≈ 3.4 pm .

Alternatively, if the fundamental length scale is set by the nucleon
size (≈ 1 fm = 10⁻¹⁵ m), the range becomes ≈ 8.8 fm. Both lie in or near
the nuclear‑interaction regime, making the Yukawa tail a plausible
carrier of a long‑range nuclear force.

A.3 Connection to Schwinger's Long‑Range Nuclear Force

In a series of papers beginning in the 1960s 8, Schwinger argued that
the empirical phenomenology of nuclear forces — particularly the
existence of a repulsive core and the systematics of binding energies —
cannot be fully explained by one‑pion exchange alone within conventional
QCD. He proposed additional long‑range components arising from a massive
vector field coupled to the nucleon current. The Ouroboros chaoiton
realises exactly this scenario: the current field J_μ is a dynamical
vector field that acquires an effective mass through the oscillatory
self‑consistent solution, and its far field produces a Yukawa potential
between nucleons. The Lagrange multiplier λ and the chaoiton frequency ω
together determine the effective mass (and thus the range) in a way that
is fixed by the internal soliton dynamics, not by an arbitrary mass
term.

A.4 Experimental Signatures via Quantum Sensing

Because the chaoiton oscillates with angular frequency ω, its far‑field
Yukawa tail is time‑varying. A force sensor operating near a nucleon or
a nucleus should therefore detect a periodically modulated Yukawa‑type
interaction. The relevant frequency ω in natural units (with L₀ ≈ λ_C)
is ω ≈ 0.9 c/λ_C ≈ 0.9 × (7.76 × 10²⁰ rad/s) ≈ 7 × 10²⁰ rad/s,
corresponding to a photon energy ≈ ħω ≈ 0.5 MeV. This lies in the
gamma‑ray regime, far above the typical operating band of mechanical
force sensors. However, the envelope of the far field, or the
time‑averaged static component, is accessible to precision force
measurements.

Quantum‑sensing platforms capable of detecting weak, short‑range spin‑
and mass‑dependent forces include:

- Cold‑atom interferometers (e.g., Raman‑transition gradiometers
  sensitive to forces at the 10⁻⁹ g level),

- Nitrogen‑vacancy (NV) centres in diamond, which can measure magnetic
  and electric field fluctuations with nanometre spatial resolution,

- Superconducting quantum interference devices (SQUIDs) and
  superconducting gravimeters, which offer exquisite sensitivity to
  time‑varying fields at low frequencies.

If the chaoiton’s effective coupling to nucleons can be estimated (e.g.,
via the interaction term J_μ A^μ, with A_μ resembling the
electromagnetic potential), one can predict the force strength and
compare it with the sensitivity limits of these devices. The
constructive outcome would be a clear exclusion plot or a detection
signal that unambiguously distinguishes a Yukawa‑type chaoiton tail from
the usual Coulomb background.

A.5 Open Questions and Next Steps

The analysis above assumes the linearised far‑field regime. A full
nonlinear matching between the inner chaoiton core and the asymptotic
Yukawa tail remains to be completed, which will refine the numerical
value of κ and its dependence on g, ω, and λ. Moreover, a definitive
identification of the fundamental length scale L₀ with a known physical
scale (e.g., the electron Compton wavelength, the nucleon charge radius,
or the Planck length) is needed before the range can be quoted in
absolute units. These tasks are part of the ongoing formal‑verification
and quantum‑phenomenology program sketched in the main text.
