Abstract

We study a classical field theory over flat (3+1)-dimensional Minkowski
space defined by two coupled covariant vector fields, Aμ and Jμ, each
subject to the Lorenz constraint, with a nonlinear coupling f(JμJμ†). We
call this the Ouroboros system. Version 1 of this record presented a
formal Lean 4 statement of the chaoiton existence theorem. Version 2
adds full numerical verification. Using the oscillatory ansatz Aμ(t,r) =
α(r)e^{−iωt}, Jμ(t,r) = β(r)e^{−iωt}, we integrate the reduced radial
ODE system using K₀ Bessel asymptotics at large r, selecting the unique
decaying (localized) solution. We then apply the full Gelfand-Fomin
conjugate point test as defined in Werbos (1998,
arXiv:patt-sol/9804003): two independent solutions of the Jacobi
perturbation equation are integrated, and the Wronskian determinant is
monitored for zero crossings. No zero crossing before r_max constitutes
a passed stability test. Across a scan of 360 parameter combinations (g,
ω, λ, A, B), 319 parameter families pass this rigorous test, confirming
the existence of stable oscillatory chaoiton solutions. The
angular-momentum-to-charge ratio L/Q ranges from 0.57 to 2.6, with the
closest approach to the electron target (L/Q = 0.5) achieved at g=0.3,
ω=2.20, λ=0.5, giving L/Q = 0.567. Stability persists over broad
parameter ranges with no fine-tuning required.

Section 2: Numerical Verification of the Existence Conditions

2.1 The Oscillatory Ansatz and Reduced ODE System

We adopt the oscillatory (chaoiton) ansatz: Aμ(t,r) = α(r)e^{−iωt},
Jμ(t,r) = β(r)e^{−iωt}, where r is the minor toroidal radius and ω > 0
is the oscillation frequency. Substituting into the Ouroboros
Euler-Lagrange equations and reducing to the static radial system, we
obtain:

α′′ + (1/r)α′ − m²_α·α + (c/2)β + 4g·α³ = 0

β′′ + (1/r)β′ − m²_β·β + (c/2)α + 4g·β³ = 0

where the effective masses are m²_α = 1 + ω² and m²_β = 1 + λ + ω², with
c = 1 (inter-field coupling, fixed by normalization), g the quartic
nonlinearity strength, and λ the H’ Lagrange multiplier coupling to the
conserved charge. The key physical observation is that ω² enters with a
positive sign, increasing the effective mass. This allows the fields to
decay exponentially at large r — evading Derrick’s theorem — while
remaining stable against radial perturbations.

2.2 Boundary Conditions and Integration Method

At large r, the nonlinear terms vanish and the fields decay as modified
Bessel functions K₀(κ·r), where κ_α = √(m²_α) and κ_β = √(m²_β). We
impose these as boundary conditions at r_max = 10 (natural units) and
integrate inward to r₀ = 0.02 using the DOP853 adaptive Runge-Kutta
integrator with relative tolerance 10^{−10} and absolute tolerance
10^{−12}. This inward integration automatically selects the decaying
solution, suppressing the growing I₀ mode. Localization is confirmed by
verifying that max(|α(r_max)|, |β(r_max)|) < 0.05.

2.3 The Gelfand-Fomin Conjugate Point Test

Following Werbos (1998, Section 2.6, arXiv:patt-sol/9804003), we test
stability by solving the Jacobi equation for perturbations ξ = (ξ_α,
ξ_β) about the background solution:

ξ_α′′ + (1/r)ξ_α′ = Q₁₁·ξ_α + Q₁₂·ξ_β

ξ_β′′ + (1/r)ξ_β′ = Q₁₂·ξ_α + Q₂₂·ξ_β

where the effective potential matrix elements evaluated on the
background solution are: Q₁₁(r) = m²_α − 12g·α(r)², Q₂₂(r) = m²_β −
12g·β(r)², Q₁₂ = −c/4.

We solve this system twice with two independent initial conditions: IC1
= (1,0,0,0) and IC2 = (0,0,1,0), and compute the Wronskian determinant
W(r) = ξ_α^(1)(r)·ξ_β^(2)(r) − ξ_α^(2)(r)·ξ_β^(1)(r). A zero crossing of
W(r) for r ∈ (r₀, r_max) constitutes a conjugate point, indicating
instability (Gelfand and Fomin, 1963, Ch. 5). No zero crossing means the
solution passes the stability test.

Section 3: Results — Stable Chaoiton Families

3.1 Summary Statistics

Parameters scanned:

- g ∈ {0.3, 0.5, 0.8, 1.0, 2.0}

- ω ∈ {1.0, 1.2, 1.5, 1.8, 2.0, 2.2}

- λ ∈ {0.0, 0.5, 1.0}

- (A, B) ∈ {(0.1,0.1), (0.1,0.2), (0.2,0.1), (0.2,0.2)}

Total combinations tested: 360. Localized solutions found: 340. Passing
full GF conjugate point test: 319.

3.2 Physical Observables

For each stable solution we compute: total energy E = 2π ∫ r·H(r) dr
(positive in all 319 cases); total charge Q = 2π ∫ r·β(r)² dr; angular
momentum L = 2π ∫ r·α(r)·β(r) dr.

The angular-momentum-to-charge ratio L/Q ranges from 0.57 to 2.60 across
the 319 stable families.

Best candidate (closest to electron target L/Q ≈ 0.5): g=0.3, ω=2.20,
λ=0.5, A=0.1, B=0.2 ⟹ L/Q = 0.567, E > 0.

Stability is not fine-tuned: broad regions of parameter space pass the
test, particularly for ω ≥ 1.5 and g ≤ 1.0.

3.3 Significance

These 319 families constitute the first rigorously verified examples of
stable, oscillatory, nontopological localized solutions in a
relativistic two-vector-field theory. The stability is established not
by a simplified surrogate criterion but by the exact Gelfand-Fomin
conjugate point test as specified in Werbos (1998). The existence of
these solutions confirms the central claim of the Lean 4 formal
statement in Section 1: that the Ouroboros system admits stable chaoiton
solutions with positive energy, finite charge, and nonzero angular
momentum.

Section 4: Connection to Prior Stability Theory

The Gelfand-Fomin test applied here is the exact method proposed in
Werbos (1998, arXiv:patt-sol/9804003, Section 2.6) for testing Strong
Second-Order Stability (SSOS) of soliton solutions in Lagrangian field
theories. That paper also introduced the complementary Bellman dynamic
programming test (Section 2.6.3), which gives consistent results with
the GF test across all 319 stable families.

The oscillatory ansatz that enables stability is consistent with the H’
stability mechanism of Werbos (1998, Section 2.7): the frequency ω plays
the role of the Lagrange multiplier λ in H’ = H + λQ, shifting the
effective mass of both fields and providing the confining potential that
the static (ω=0) system lacks. The static system fails the GF test; the
oscillatory system passes it robustly.

This confirms the prediction in Werbos (1993, Chaos Solitons Fractals
3:321) that chaotic solitons (chaoitons) — stable oscillatory attractors
in conservative field theories — are physically realizable, and provides
the first concrete numerical example in a relativistic vector field
theory.
